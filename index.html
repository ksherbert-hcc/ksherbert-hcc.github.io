A Musing on Physics

Theoretical physicists tend to approach their field from the perspective that all physics can eventually be reduced to a set of postulates. Moreover, this set of postulates is in some sense beautiful. Every physicist has their own sense of beauty, perhaps, but most will agree that the postulates shouldn't feel arbitrary. The laws of physics should make sense, from a certain detached perspective. It is popular, for instance, to claim that quantum mechanics doesn't make sense, from the perspective of we human beings who operate at human-sized space and time scales. But I feel on the contrary that no other branch of physics makes more sense, from the perspective of linear algebra which is one of the only fields we humans well and truly know.

A key distinction is that the truly beautiful theory closes its eyes to the world as it is, and considers, at a fundamental level, the world as it should be. We are of course inspired by empiricism to refine our sense of beauty. Only by observing the astonishing interaction between electricity and magnetism did the 19th century physicsists realize that the two could be unified into one beautiful whole, and only be observing the astonishing interaction between electrons and magnets did the 20th century physicists start to uncover the even simpler beauty that the whole human world could be expressed seamlessly in the language of linear algebra. Taking into account the lessons from the world above our heads - Einstein's developments in relativity theory - allowed us to formulate quantum field theory, arguably the most successful a priori science of all time.

Nearly every aspect of this theory is beautiful. With it, we predict the most fundamental interactions of nature to an astonishing degree of precision - the beauty of truth. And yet, it retains also the beauty of discovery: nature still continues to surprise us with new physical interactions. While lesser theories would require ad hoc cases for every special context, epicycles upon epicycles, quantum field theory integrate the new interaction into the fold of unified beauty simply by inferring a new, distinct "field" which is and always has been - beautiful in its uniqueness. And yet every time we do this, we discover that even these distinct fields are related to one another through some even more unified mathematical structure - beautiful in its solidarity.

Nearly every aspect.

There is one small catch to the whole framework of quantum field theory. A single pesky integral which should result in an infinite mass, but, empirically, does not. One small blight on the whole theory. Perhaps it will be overcome one day. But for now, how do we resolve it..? We simply replace the integral with the empirical mass. There is - as of yet - no mathematical structure. It is a purely arbitrary number, as far as the theory is concerned. Doing that makes everything work perfectly. But it all rests on this single a posteriori detail. This is the most successful physical theory of all time, and even here, we are forced - for now - to remember this humbling fact - it is the physical world which explains the mathematical world. Not the other way around.
